# Maison Move Further Research: Customer & Market Analysis

---

## 1. Understanding the Customer

### Your Core Audience (Confirmed)
You already know her well because she is you. But here's the data-backed profile:

**Primary: The "Studio-to-Street" Woman**
- Age: 20-30
- UK-based, urban or semi-urban
- Trains 3-5x per week (weights, pilates, yoga, running)
- Active social life (coffee, brunch, dinners, travel)
- Instagram and TikTok native — influenced by what she sees on social media (nearly 70% of women cite social media as a purchasing influence for activewear)
- Spends on clothes before saving — values looking good and feeling good
- Currently wears Gymshark, Oner Active, Skims lounge, Zara, H&M basics
- Frustrated by the gap between gym clothes and going-out clothes
- Price range: willing to pay £65-£150 for pieces that genuinely replace two items in her wardrobe

### Audiences You Might Be Missing

**The 30-40 Professional Woman**
- Data shows women aged 35+ are the highest spenders on new clothing purchases in the UK
- She's the same woman, just with more disposable income
- Trains before or after work, needs to transition seamlessly
- Less price-sensitive than the 20-30 bracket
- Prioritises quality and longevity over trend
- More likely to discover brands through press, word of mouth, or boutique stockists than TikTok
- **Why she matters:** She'll spend more per order, return less, and become a loyal repeat customer

**The "Wellness Mum"**
- Age: 28-40
- Trains when she can (gym, home workouts, yoga, walks)
- Needs versatile pieces that work for the school run, a quick session, and coffee after
- Values comfort, quality, and not looking like she's "in gym clothes"
- MineralSoft recovery fabric is a genuine selling point for post-natal bodies
- **Why she matters:** Huge untapped market for premium activewear that doesn't look like activewear

**The Luxury Loungewear Buyer**
- Not necessarily a gym-goer
- Buys Skims, COS, Arket for comfort pieces
- Would buy Maison Move lounge sets purely for the fabric quality and aesthetic
- Responds to the "quiet luxury" positioning
- **Why she matters:** Expands your addressable market beyond gym girls

### Key Stats
- Women accounted for 47.98% of activewear revenue in 2025 (the largest segment)
- Gym and fitness use accounts for 46.17% of women's activewear purchases
- Premium activewear is growing at 9.06% CAGR (2026-2031) — faster than mass market
- Mass market still dominates at 66.87% of sales — meaning premium is the growth opportunity

## 2. Has This Been Done Before?

### The Short Answer
The "studio-to-street" concept exists. The technology angle combined with fashion-house positioning does not. Nobody has combined all three: multi-zone compression + recovery fabric + temperature regulation, wrapped in a luxury aesthetic at a bridge price point.

### Brands That Have Tried Similar Concepts

**Outdoor Voices (US) — FAILED**
- Raised $60M+, valued at $100M in 2018
- Closed all stores by March 2024, acquired through quasi-bankruptcy
- **What went wrong:**
  - Overspent on customer acquisition (burning cash on marketing)
  - No genuine technical differentiation — just "nice looking" activewear
  - Founder mismanagement and internal chaos
  - Tried to be everything to everyone instead of owning a niche
  - No proprietary technology or fabric innovation
- **Lesson for Maison Move:** Technology is your moat. Outdoor Voices had no real reason to exist beyond aesthetics. You have three proprietary fabric technologies that create genuine differentiation.

**Sweaty Betty (UK) — STRUGGLING**
- Reported £13.4M pre-tax loss in 2023
- Turnover declined from £167.7M to £144.2M
- **What went wrong:**
  - Acquired by Wolverine Worldwide for $410M — then parent company struggled
  - Lost its premium positioning by discounting too heavily
  - No genuine fabric innovation — competes on brand and design only
  - Overexpanded into retail locations
- **Lesson for Maison Move:** Don't discount. Don't overexpand into physical retail too early. Your technology justifies the price — protect that.

**Gymshark (UK) — SUCCEEDING BUT VULNERABLE**
- Proposed restructure in April 2025, putting a third of workforce at risk
- Dominates the 18-25 "gym bro/gym girl" market
- **Gap they leave open:** Gymshark is loud, trend-driven, and youth-focused. They don't serve the woman who wants something elevated. They have zero fabric technology claims.
- **Lesson for Maison Move:** You're not competing with Gymshark. You're serving the woman who's grown out of Gymshark.

**Lululemon — THE BENCHMARK**
- Successfully owns "studio-to-street" at a premium price
- Strong proprietary fabrics (Nulu, Everlux, Luxtreme)
- **Gap they leave open:** Price point is higher (£88-£128 for leggings). No recovery or temperature-regulation technology. Perceived as "basic" by younger consumers. Weak in the UK market compared to North America.
- **Lesson for Maison Move:** Proprietary fabric names work. Lululemon proved that naming your technology (Nulu, Everlux) builds perceived value. You're doing this with BodyMap, MineralSoft, ClimateKnit.

### Why Nobody Has Combined All Three Technologies
1. **Cost barrier:** Each technology (compression mapping, mineral-infused fabric, PCM temperature regulation) adds cost. Combining all three is expensive.
2. **Knowledge barrier:** Most activewear founders come from fashion or marketing, not textile engineering. They don't know these technologies exist.
3. **Manufacturing complexity:** You need different specialist suppliers or a full-package manufacturer who understands functional textiles.
4. **The "good enough" trap:** Most brands think nice fabric + nice design = premium. They don't invest in genuine functional innovation because the market doesn't demand it yet.

**This is your opportunity.** The market hasn't demanded it because nobody has shown them what's possible.
