## 1. BodyMap — Multi-Zone Adaptive Compression

### How It Works
Precision knitting machines vary the density, weight, and knit structure across a single garment. Different zones use different compression levels — firm around the core and glutes, lighter at the calves, breathable mesh behind the knees. This requires specialised seamless knitting equipment (typically Santoni machines).

### Downsides
- **Manufacturing cost:** Requires advanced seamless knitting machines. Not every factory has them. Adds 20-30% to production cost vs standard leggings.
- **Size grading complexity:** Compression zones need to be re-mapped for every size. A zone that hits the knee on a size S won't hit the knee on a size XL without separate grading.
- **Fit testing is critical:** If zones are misaligned, it creates discomfort rather than support. Requires extensive sampling and fit testing across the full size range.
- **Limited manufacturer options:** Only factories with Santoni or similar machines can produce this. Limits your supplier options.

### Solutions
- **Start with 2-3 zone designs, not 4.** Simpler to manufacture, easier to grade, lower risk of misalignment.
- **Work with a manufacturer who already produces compression sportswear** (e.g., factories that make for Gymshark, 2XU, or Zone3 already have the machines).
- **Invest in fit testing on real bodies across your full size range.** Budget 2-3 rounds of sampling before committing to bulk.
- **Consider seamless knit for leggings and sports bras only.** Other pieces (joggers, half-zip, lounge set) don't need zoned compression and can be cut-and-sew, which is cheaper and easier.

## 2. MineralSoft — Recovery-Infused Fabric

### How It Works
Fabric is treated with mineral-infused microcapsules (bioceramics) that absorb body heat and re-emit it as far-infrared energy. This is claimed to increase blood flow and aid muscle recovery. Silver-ion treatment is added for antimicrobial (freshness) properties.

### What the Science Actually Says
The evidence is **mixed but promising:**
- A 2021 systematic review in PLOS One found results were "inconclusive" overall but noted FIR garments "may be of interest mainly through their effects on thermoregulation and haemodynamic function."
- A 2024 study found FIR garments "can enhance recovery following a resistance exercise bout" with improvements in neuromuscular performance.
- Another study found FIR clothing "did not facilitate recovery after maximal eccentric exercise" over 72 hours.
- **Bottom line:** There's enough science to support the claim, but don't overstate it. Use language like "designed to support recovery" rather than "clinically proven to recover muscles."

### Downsides
- **Wash durability:** Mineral coatings and microcapsules degrade with washing. Topical applications (printed on) last approximately 50 washes. Embedded in the fibre lasts longer but costs more.
- **Efficacy debate:** The science is not conclusive. Making strong medical claims could cause regulatory issues (UK ASA advertising standards).
- **Cost:** Adding mineral-infused treatment increases fabric cost by approximately 15-25%.
- **Supplier dependency:** You need a fabric supplier who specialises in functional treatments. Not standard.

### Solutions
- **Use fibre-embedded minerals rather than topical coatings** where possible. They last longer and maintain efficacy through more wash cycles.
- **Keep marketing language careful.** Say "recovery-infused" and "designed to support recovery" rather than "heals" or "cures." This keeps you compliant with UK advertising standards.
- **Include a care guide** that protects longevity: wash cold, no fabric softener, no tumble dry. This preserves the treatment AND positions the product as premium.
- **Get independent testing done.** Even a small-scale test from a UK sports science lab adds credibility and gives you marketing content.

## 3. Silver-Ion Antimicrobial Technology (Deep Dive)

### How It Works
Silver ions (Ag+) are embedded into the fabric fibre or applied as a treatment. When bacteria land on the fabric, the silver ions penetrate the microbial cells and bind to essential proteins, disrupting their energy production and reproduction. Result: the fabric stays fresh and odour-free for significantly longer than untreated fabric.

### Key Benefits
- Stops 99.9% of bacterial growth on the fabric
- Reduces odour between washes
- Extends garment lifespan (fewer washes needed)
- Remains at least 95% effective after 20 washes (fibre-embedded)
- Works continuously — doesn't need activation

### Downsides
- **Cost increase:** 30-50% more expensive to manufacture than standard fabric
- **Wash degradation:** Effectiveness reduces over time. Topical treatments degrade faster than fibre-embedded.
- **Environmental concerns:** Silver nanoparticles released during washing can enter waterways. Some environmental groups have raised concerns.
- **Health concerns at high concentrations:** At normal textile levels it's safe, but excessive silver contact can theoretically cause argyria (skin discolouration) — extremely rare and only at industrial exposure levels.
- **Regulatory:** Some markets are tightening regulations on nanosilver in textiles. EU and UK currently allow it but worth monitoring.

### Solutions
- **Use fibre-embedded silver-ion rather than topical coating.** Lasts longer, more durable, less environmental leaching.
- **Dose appropriately.** You don't need maximum silver loading. Work with your fabric supplier to find the optimal balance of efficacy, cost, and longevity.
- **Highlight the sustainability angle:** Fewer washes = less water, less energy, less microplastic shedding. Position silver-ion as an eco-benefit, not just a freshness feature.
- **Get Oeko-Tex 100 certification** on finished garments (not just fabric). This certifies the silver levels are safe for skin contact.

## 4. ClimateKnit — Phase-Change Material (PCM) Technology

### How It Works
Microencapsulated phase-change materials (mPCMs) are embedded into the fabric. These microcapsules contain substances that absorb excess body heat by changing from solid to liquid (like wax melting). When you cool down, they release that stored heat by solidifying again. Originally developed for military and space applications.

### Downsides
- **Temporary effect:** The warming/cooling effect is temporary. Once all the PCM has changed phase, it stops regulating until you cool down/warm up again. It's a buffer, not a thermostat.
- **Adding more PCM = heavier fabric + higher cost + lower durability.** There's a ceiling on how much you can add before the fabric becomes stiff or heavy.
- **Wash durability:** Microcapsules can break down with repeated washing. Encapsulation quality varies by supplier.
- **Cost:** PCM treatment adds significant cost. One of the most expensive functional textile technologies.
- **Scalability:** Difficult and expensive to produce in large quantities at consistent quality.
- **Long-term stability:** Repeated thermal cycling can degrade PCM performance over time.

### Solutions
- **Use PCM selectively, not across the entire garment.** Apply it only where it matters most — high-heat zones (underarms, lower back, behind knees). This reduces cost and weight.
- **Be honest in marketing.** Call it "temperature-regulating" not "temperature-controlling." Explain it buffers heat rather than eliminates it.
- **Consider making ClimateKnit a feature of specific pieces only** (the Half-Zip, for example) rather than the entire collection. This keeps costs manageable and makes those pieces feel special.
- **Source from established PCM suppliers** with good encapsulation quality (Outlast is the best-known, but there are Chinese suppliers with comparable quality at lower cost).

## 5. The Celliant Print Hack

### What Is Celliant?
Celliant is the market leader in infrared textile technology. They embed natural bioceramic minerals into fibres, yarns, and fabrics. Their "Celliant Print" product is a topical application — bioceramic mineral powder screen-printed directly onto any fabric type.

### The "Print Hack"
Celliant Print is significant because:
- **It can be applied to ANY fabric type** — you don't need special yarn or fibres
- **It's a cost-effective entry point** compared to full fibre-embedded Celliant
- **Higher mineral loading is possible** with print vs embedded, meaning stronger infrared effect
- **Lasts approximately 50 washes** — the useful life of most activewear

### The Catch
**Celliant requires a brand licensing agreement.** You cannot buy Celliant powder and apply it yourself. You must become a licensed brand partner.

### What This Means for Maison Move
You have two paths:

**Option A: License Celliant**
- Approach Celliant directly as a brand partner
- Use their Print technology on your MineralSoft pieces
- You get their clinical studies, testing data, and marketing support
- Cost: licensing fee + per-unit cost for the print treatment
- Benefit: credibility, proven technology, existing research you can cite

**Option B: Source your own bioceramic mineral treatment**
- Work with a Chinese fabric supplier who offers generic far-infrared mineral coatings
- Cheaper, no licensing fee
- Less clinical backing — you'd need to commission your own testing
- Risk: quality variance between suppliers
- Suppliers like Shaoxing Starke and Xiamen Newrgy offer these treatments

**Recommendation:** Start with Option B for your initial collection to keep costs down. If the technology resonates with customers, pursue a Celliant license for future collections to add credibility.

## 6. How to Make Claims Without Getting in Trouble

The UK Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) is strict about health and performance claims.

**Safe to say:**
- "Designed to support recovery"
- "Recovery-infused fabric"
- "Temperature-regulating technology"
- "Engineered to stay fresh"
- "Anti-microbial properties"

**Avoid saying:**
- "Clinically proven to aid recovery" (unless you have your own clinical study)
- "Heals muscles" or "cures soreness"
- "Regulates your body temperature" (implies medical device)
- "Anti-bacterial" as a health claim

Keep it aspirational and descriptive, not medical.
