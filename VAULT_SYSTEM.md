# MaisonMove — The Vault System

## What Is a Vault?

A Vault is a numbered, limited-edition product collection released as a timed drop. It is the core commercial mechanic of MaisonMove — how the brand sells, scales, builds community, and funds itself.

Every Vault is:

- **Numbered** — each unit carries a printed edition number (e.g., "047/250") on the inner waistband label
- **Limited** — 250-2,000 units per Vault, sized to pre-validated demand from the waitlist
- **Final sale** — no returns, no exchanges. Standard in limited-run drop culture and critical to the financial model.
- **DTC-only** — available exclusively through maisonmove.com. No wholesale, no marketplaces, no third parties.
- **Technology-forward** — each Vault introduces or layers a new textile technology on top of the previous stack
- **Named** — Vaults carry collection names, not product codes. They are chapters in a brand story, not inventory.
- **Permanent** — there will never be another Vault 001. Each is a one-time production run. The sold-out page stays live as an archive.

---

## Why Vaults Work

The vault model simultaneously solves the six problems that kill bootstrapped activewear brands:

| Problem | How Vaults Solve It |
|---|---|
| **Cash flow risk** on $10-20K capital | 250 units at ~£20 COGS = ~£5,000 production cost. Fundable via pre-orders. Revenue collected before manufacturing. |
| **Returns destroying margin** | Final sale eliminates the 20-30% return rate that kills DTC apparel. Returns cost exactly £0. |
| **No brand recognition** | Scarcity creates perceived value. A 250-unit sellout generates more press, social proof, and FOMO than a 5,000-unit launch sitting at 40% sold. |
| **Expensive fabric technology** | Premium pricing (£89-95) is justified by exclusivity + technology. Customers pay more for something they might not get. |
| **Sizing uncertainty** | Small runs collect real sizing data before committing to large production. Waitlist sign-ups include "What size do you typically wear?" |
| **Manufacturer MOQ pressure** | 250-500 units falls within the sweet spot for low-MOQ activewear manufacturers. No need for 1,000+ unit commitments. |

Each Vault carries a collection name that reinforces collectibility and brand mythology:

```

VAULT 003 — "Thermal"

VAULT 006 — ...
```

The numbering is permanent and sequential. Buyers of early Vaults are founding members of the brand. The "Founding 250" (Vault 001 buyers) have a permanent identity within the community and receive early access to every subsequent Vault.

## Size Ratio Strategy

Standard activewear distribution (adjust based on actual waitlist data):

| Size | % of Run | Units (of 250) | Units (of 500) | Units (of 1,000) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| XS | 8-10% | ~22 | ~45 | ~90 |
| S | 20-22% | ~52 | ~105 | ~210 |
| M | 30-32% | ~78 | ~155 | ~310 |
| L | 25-27% | ~65 | ~130 | ~260 |
| XL | 12-15% | ~33 | ~65 | ~130 |

**Critical:** Collect "What size do you typically wear?" on the waitlist sign-up form. If the audience skews older (likely with Ruth Jones's demographic), expect heavier M/L/XL distribution than standard. Use real data, not industry averages, to set ratios from Vault 002 onward.

## Access Tiers

Every Vault uses a staggered release to create layered urgency and reward loyalty:

| Tier | Access Window | Who |
|---|---|---|
| **Founding 250** | -24 hours | Vault 001 buyers (permanent early access for life) |
| **Vault alumni** | -12 hours | Anyone who has bought a previous Vault |
| **Waitlist** | 0 hours (drop time) | Email waitlist subscribers |
| **General** | +24 hours | Open to everyone |

This means by Vault 003, the Founding 250 have a full 24-hour head start. Most popular sizes may be gone before general release. This drives waitlist sign-ups ("I missed it because I wasn't on the list") and rewards early customers.

## The VAT Cliff

The UK VAT registration threshold is **£90,000** in rolling 12-month taxable turnover (possibly dropping to £60-70K from April 2026). MaisonMove will hit this during or shortly after Vault 002.

### What Happens

Once registered, you must charge 20% VAT on all UK sales.

| Scenario | Retail Price | Your Revenue Per Unit | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-VAT | £89 | £89.00 | Full margin |
| Add VAT on top | £106.80 | £89.00 | Customer pays more. May hurt conversion. |
| Absorb VAT | £89 | £74.17 | Margin drops from 77.5% to 73.0% |
| Raise price to offset | £95 | £79.17 | Split the difference. Slight increase, slight margin hit. |

### Recommendation

Price at £89 pre-VAT for Vaults 001-002. When VAT registration triggers, raise to £95 and absorb the remainder. The technology and brand equity justify the £6 uplift by that point. The audience is already anchored to the value proposition.

**Do not** try to suppress revenue to stay below the threshold. HMRC actively monitors for this behaviour.

### VAT Upside

Once registered, you reclaim VAT on all business purchases (manufacturing, packaging, tools, professional services). On ~£118K of annual variable costs, that's roughly **£19,700 reclaimed** — partially offsetting the revenue impact.

## Cash Flow Timing

Money in and money out don't happen simultaneously. At $10-20K starting capital, cash flow timing is the real constraint.

### Traditional Model (Manufacture Then Sell)

```
Week 1-2:     Pay manufacturer deposit (50%)           -£2,500
Week 3-8:     Production (waiting, no revenue)              £0
Week 8:       Pay manufacturer balance (50%)           -£2,500
Week 8:       Pay packaging supplier                     -£375
Week 9:       Receive goods, QC inspection                   £0
Week 9-10:    Vault drops, revenue comes in           +£22,250
Week 10:      Shopify payout (2-3 business days)
Week 10-11:   Ship all orders                            -£875
Week 11:      Payment processing fees settled            -£508

Cash-negative window: Weeks 1-9 (£5,375 out of pocket)
Final net cash: +£15,492
```

### Pre-Order Model (Sell Then Manufacture) — RECOMMENDED

```
Week 1:       Open pre-orders on maisonmove.com
Week 1-2:     Revenue collected via Shopify          +£22,250
Week 2:       Shopify payout received
Week 2:       Pay manufacturer deposit (50%)           -£2,500
Week 4-9:     Production
Week 9:       Pay manufacturer balance (50%)           -£2,500
Week 9:       Pay packaging supplier                     -£375
Week 10:      Receive goods, QC, ship                    -£875
Week 10:      Processing fees settled                    -£508

Cash-negative window: NEVER
Earliest positive cash position: Week 2
```

**The pre-order model means you are never cash-negative.** This is why "never build inventory you haven't already sold" is a financial survival rule, not a preference.

### Communication for Pre-Orders

Customers will accept a 6-8 week wait between ordering and shipping if you:
- State the timeline clearly at checkout ("Ships by [date]")
- Send a production update email at Week 4 ("Your leggings are in production")
- Send a shipping confirmation with tracking
- Position the wait as part of the exclusivity ("Each Vault is made to order for our community")

1. **Final sale = zero returns cost.** Lululemon, Oner Active, and Gymshark absorb 20-30% return rates. Each return costs £8-15 in processing. On a £50 average item, that's effectively a 3-9% margin hit across the business.

2. **Pre-order funding = zero inventory risk.** Traditional brands carry dead stock. Every unsold unit erodes margin. MaisonMove only manufactures what's already paid for.

3. **Scarcity pricing = premium justified.** An £89 legging in perpetual stock needs to compete on value. An £89 legging in a numbered run of 250 competes on access.

## Financial Rules

These rules protect the vault system's economic model:

1. **Size production to waitlist, not ambition.** If the waitlist suggests 200 sellable units, produce 200 — not 300 hoping for walk-ins. A smaller sellout beats a larger partial sell-through every time.

2. **Pre-orders fund production.** Collect revenue before committing to manufacturing. Never be cash-negative.

3. **Final sale is non-negotiable for Vaults.** The moment you accept returns on limited drops, you introduce 20-30% cost leakage and inventory uncertainty. The model doesn't work with returns.

4. **20% of capital stays untouched.** The contingency fund exists for costs you didn't anticipate. It is not a budget line to be spent — it's insurance.

5. **Never discount a Vault.** No sale prices, no promo codes, no influencer discounts. Every unit sells at full price or doesn't sell. Discounting trains customers to wait and destroys the urgency that drives sellouts.

6. **VAT-register proactively.** Don't wait for HMRC to chase you. Register when you see the threshold approaching, adjust pricing cleanly, and reclaim input VAT from day one of registration.

7. **Reinvest 40-50% of Year 1 profit into Year 2 growth.** Community events, better photography, trade show attendance, team hires. The vault system generates exceptional margins — use them to build the brand, not just accumulate cash.

8. **Wholesale revenue never exceeds 40% of total.** When the Selfridges tier launches (Year 3+), DTC vaults must remain the majority of revenue. DTC margins (75%+) fund the business. Wholesale margins (~48%) build prestige.

## Why the Vault System Works Financially

The vault model's financial power comes from three compounding effects:

**1. Pre-order funding eliminates working capital risk.**
You are never cash-negative if you collect before you manufacture. Traditional apparel brands need £50-200K in working capital to fund production runs months before revenue. MaisonMove needs £0.

**2. Final sale eliminates returns cost.**
The 20-30% return rate that destroys normal DTC apparel brands costs MaisonMove exactly £0. On Year 1 revenue of £440K, a 25% return rate at £12/return would cost a normal brand ~£13,200 in logistics alone, plus the margin loss on unsold returned inventory. MaisonMove avoids this entirely.

**3. Scarcity justifies premium pricing.**
At £89 retail with £20 COGS, MaisonMove runs 77.5% gross margin — higher than Lululemon (~59%), Gymshark (~67%), or Oner Active (~68%). This is possible because the limited-edition model supports pricing that perpetual inventory cannot. Customers are paying for access as much as product.

These three factors together mean the vault system is structurally profitable unless the brand fails to build an audience. The entire financial risk reduces to one question: **can you get enough people to care about 250 units?**

Everything after that is compounding.
